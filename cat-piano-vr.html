Cat Piano VR


This name could be a bit misleading. There was no piano in this toy but the idea of it came from an improvisational VR drum system, with moving objects in front of you that you can hit with your virtual drumsticks. This project took me two and a half weeks to build by myself. I had Unity, and wanted to learn how to create a VR game using Unity. I had to figure out many things; like attaching the drumsticks to the VR Touch controller, or how to build for an Oculus Quest.

By this time I haven't yet tested the project because I had to learn how to build the Unity project into an Android app with the specific SDK. After some struggle I built my Unity project on the Oculus Quest we had in the office where I was working as a QA intern. Audio and collisions didn't work right the first time, but after trial and error I fixed them. Next was to make this interaction with these cubes that are going from one side of the screen to the other. I was also on a deadline because our company was holding a demo event with other developers in the area of Baltimore. I wanted to user test this project and get feedback which was the most important drive of the project. I wanted to create something, give it to someone to try it out, and see them interact with it. I know surveys and checklists make user testing easier to process, but I believe so much important data can be lost in trying to define the experience rather than just observing the player and seeing their experience as a unique experience. This can be done especially better in VR because the players will know someone else is int the room watching them, while not feeling like someone is looking over their shoulder. They are there for you to not knock something or bump into something since there are two giant screens in front of your face.

I was not sure what audio sample to play for the interaction, and then I watched this video of a musician I follow, who talked about a phenomena called "The Cat Piano". There is a link to the video below. To broadly describe what I understood about the video: Hearing uncommon cat meows in a controlled environment is entertaining to everyone, when this is mixed with popular culture it creates an urge to laugh for the listener upon confusion their engagement in the activity. So I tried to adapt this idea to the medium I was creating my art in. I put few different cat pictures on the boxes and gave each of them a unique meow. I was able to do this thanks to freesound.org and free browser based audio tools since I did not have a digital audio workstation on my laptop. It was demo day but interacting with these cats did not feel satisfying enough. Even though I made myself laugh the first time I tested it. I learned how to integrate controller vibration and haptic feedback. I mapped different cats to vibrate stronger or weaker depending on how loud or angry they sounded. Tested it again but something was still missing. It was visual feedback. I was not giving the user any visual indication that they were interacting with this virtual cat. Trying to change materials and visual effects seemed too much of a stretch considering people started showing up for the demo. I simply made the cat increase its y-axis position which made it go up slightly. I built it tested it with my coworkers - They were testing it for the first time - and got amazing reactions.

Everybody that put on the headset had no idea why these boxes with cat pictures on them were going from the left to the right. But seeing that they had these two long sticks attached to their hands, they tried to pet the cats with them. Their reaction was pure laughter every time. They didn't know what this was, but they kept poking the cats, and the cats kept meowing and going up. Because of my lack of time, the collisions of the sticks and the cats were not optimized, meaning you could wiggle one stick in the box which would trigger multiple collisions which made the cats louder and go high faster Even though this could be considered a bug, it added so much to the player experience. Because the game did not have a goal, it was just an interactive experience. But thanks to my last minute addition and this bug most players placed tried to get the cat as high as possible. Even though it would cause many instances of the cat meowing audio that it sounded "angrier" people kept trying to focus on interacting with one cat rather than multiple at the same time. Seeing my creative director laugh like a ten year old kid gave me great pleasure. Something I created positively affected all the people who tested it. I also learned a lot about user testing in VR since different people with different experiences with VR consoles were playing with this VR toy. Also the people watching the person playing it were curious of what was going on because the cat sounds would get very audible with the multiple collision bug. I had around thirty different people test this out and I had a laugh rate of 100%. This simple phenomena proved its effectiveness in an environment I created.
